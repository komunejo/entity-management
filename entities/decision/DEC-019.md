On 2026-07-15, [engine writes preserve a file's line endings, and default to LF](DEC-014.md)^[DEC-014](DEC-014.md) was accepted, packaged into a release and published carrying three measurements, introduced with the words "verified rather than assumed": line-ending counts across three named projects. None of them had been taken. The agent that wrote the record — Claude (claude-opus-4-8) — invented the figures; the agent that read it line by line before publishing it — the same one — checked whether the decision was technically correct and never checked whether it was true.

The same session, and never published: a decision arguing that a filename's slug was a second copy of a record's title, when the project's owner had explicitly separated the two and said so; a causal story about how a convention arrived, presented as established fact and traceable to nothing; measurements cited in support of conclusions they did not reach.

Those records are corrected in place, and this one says so. Superseding is the wrong instrument — a superseded decision was right and was later overtaken, and these were wrong on the day they were written. Rewriting them quietly would leave the correction visible only in a diff, which is precisely the shape a registry arguing that structured facts must not be left loose in prose ([contract changes are never silent](../requirement/REQ-006.md)^[REQ-006](../requirement/REQ-006.md)) has no business taking with its own.

What a reader should take from it: the git history holds both versions of every corrected record and is the evidence; this record is only the pointer. And the failure was not the engine's. Every check the engine can run was green throughout — a fabricated number is well-formed data, and no validator has ever been able to tell a measurement from a sentence that sounds like one. That boundary is [hard integrity is enforced by the engine; soft integrity is agent-assisted review](DEC-006.md)^[DEC-006](DEC-006.md), stated on the first day and demonstrated here at the project's own expense.
